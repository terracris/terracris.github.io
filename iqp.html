Co-creating Nature Based Solutions in Divjaka, Albania

Description

This project was an environmental project, where I and three other students collaboratively discovered and created solutions incorporating nature to mitigate soil erosion of precious watersheds in Albania (abroad). Our project was a part of a grant formulated by a German Governmental Organization called GIZ that provides aid to other countries. The regional watershed, which includes the Karavasta lagoon within the park, and the Shkumbini River along the northern border of the Karavasta National Park, provides important resources to the people living there. Fishermen will fish throughout the lagoon, farmers work the land near the river, and tourists come to visit the beaches and uncommon birds that can be found there. All these groups are being affected by erosion and drought. Coastal erosion creates blockages in the lagoon's channels, reducing or destroying communication with the sea. This leads to decreased oxygen levels and increased salinity within the lagoon's waters, harming fish populations, which in turn harms fishermen and birds. Riparian erosion destroys farmland and intensifies flooding. Because of the hazards around Divjaka, GIZ is interested in creating a few NbS that could have a positive holistic effect on the region. They also want these NbS to be designed in such a way that they could potentially be implemented elsewhere at a later point in time, with minimal modifications. GIZ's past work in Albania has included ecosystem-based adaptation projects in Elbasan, and flood mitigation plans in Shkoder.

Execution

So where did we start? My team and I did some preliminary research on the hazards present in Divjaka, Albania before being physically on sight. Once we arrived to Albania we had several semi-structured interviews with Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs), Government Organizations, Municipalities, Fishermen Organizations and Farmers.

From these dialogues, we were able to piece together the story of what was occuring in Divjaka and how these groups would solve these issues using nature. Additionally, we held workshops with both High School & University students from the University of Tirana to illicit more ideas.

So what was the story? Apperantly, over years, the current of the Shkumbini river was eroding the river bed. The eroded sediments were depositing onto the basin of the lagoon facing the adriatic sea. Why was this a problem? The Karavasta Lagoon had channels connecting it to the Adriatic sea which were now being clogged by the deposited sediments. Lagoons contain brakish water, that is, a mixture of freshwater and salt water. If the channels are blocked, Albania's hot summers will result in evaporation of the water increasing salinity levels. This is detrimental to wildlife present in the area. This has resulted in a decrease in the population of fish affecting the livelihoods of fishermen. In addition, the loss of land to farmers along the river has decreased their yields significantly, again, affecting their livelihood. You can see in the timelapse the progressive deposition of sediments and the change in the shape of the watershed.

As a result of our dialogues and activities, we collaboratively designed nature based solutions using reparian forest buffers, rock armor, and vegetation/shrubbery along the river bed. A reparian forest buffer is a segment of trees placed along the river bed. The roots from the trees help reinforce the river bed and act as a buffer for strong currents and floods. Lining the bed of the river with rocks decreases stream velocity minimizing eroding. Additionally, if we planted vegetation below the rock armor that would anchor sediments resulting in additional fortification.

We conducted a transect walk along the river with a local farmer to discover where we should place our nature based solutions.

From our transect walk, we discovered the locations that would be ideal to place these interventions. We also were able to uncover what tree we should use for the Riparian Forest Buffer. The Stone Pine Tree is an excellent choice since it has a natural resistance to environments with a moderate-high salinity.

What did I learn?

Everyone is an expert in their field after enough time doing it. We noticed that there was no difference in discussion between the fishermen and members of government organizations. The fishermen in fact had more knowledge about the state of the lagoon and additional ideas of how to address the issues at hand. In addition, I learned how to talk with various stakeholders and facilitate discussions to illicit stakeholder needs and requirements. Lastly, I have learned about generating designs using nature. Lastly, I learned about the hospitality of Albanian culture. Albanian's are extremely welcoming and I would highly encourage visiting! If you know even a few Albanian phrases, they will love you! I found myself learning a decent amount of Albanian to hold a light conversation.
