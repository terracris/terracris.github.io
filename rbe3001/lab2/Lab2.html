Introduction

In this lab, our team implemented functions for forward kinematics and used them to calculate the pose of the end-effector of the arm. Additionally, we collected and visualized data on the arm’s movements.

To calculate the forward kinematics of the robot arm, we used the Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) method. To perform the DH process, we defined reference frames for each joint, set up a parameter table, formed homogeneous transformation matrices for each joint, and created a complete transformation matrix for the end-effector with respect to the base frame. To define reference frames for each joint, we assigned the z axes, located the origin for each joint, assigned the x axes, and assigned the right-hand coordinate frames, which indicate the y axes. The parameter table included Θ, d, a, and 𝛼, where for all revolute joints, Θ is variable, and for every prismatic joint, d is variable. Using the parameter table, we plugged in each row of parameters for each joint (besides theta) into the formula:

This gave us the transformation between each frame. Then by plugging in the specific theta gained by reading the joint values, the homogeneous transformation matrices were multiplied together to get the overall transformation matrix.

Our team used the DH method to calculate the forward kinematics and implement them in functions so we could determine how far the robot moves based on the joint angles.

  3. Assign the xi

Figure 1: The robot arm configuration and dimensions, and the origins of the intermediate coordinate frames.

Step 1a: Assigning the Zi axes.

Initially, we were given the initial and final coordinate frames and had to determine the intermediate frames. The Z0 axis points upward along the axis of rotation of the revolute joint and was placed on the base of the robot. X0 was chosen arbitrarily to point leftward.  To locate the following axes of the actuators, we assigned the z axes as the axis of motion/actuation. Additionally, according to a rule of the DH method, the end-effector was assigned a z-axis parallel to the revolute joint that preceded it.  

Step 1b: Locate the origin of oi

The origins for each frame were found by finding the common normal between axes. For instance, the Z1 axis is perpendicular to Z0 and so the origin must lie at their intersection. Z1 and Z2, however, are parallel to each other. Therefore, the choice is arbitrary. Thus, we chose to place that origin at the joint since it we found the location convenient. The same was true for the Z3 and Z2 axes, and so we placed o3 at the end effector. This choice was not only out of convenience but rather because it would result in the completion of our goal. Our goal is to determine a forward kinematic solution for the end effector to the base of the robot to know where the position of the end effector each time we move. If we did not place the origin at the end effector, then we would miss our goal.

Step 1c: Assign the xi axis

We assigned the axes along the common normal between zi-1 and zi through the ith origin. Z0 and Z1 make a plane at their intersection points, therefore x would have to be normal to that plane. X is normal to that plane in two directions. We chose the direction parallel to the previous frame for convenience. Axis Z1 and Z2 are parallel to each other. Therefore the common normal between the two axes passing through their origins is along the direction of L2. We chose the upward position for convenience. lastly, axis Z2 and Z3 are parallel. Therefore the common normal between the two axes is along L3. Again, we chose the direction parallel to earlier frames for convenience. In Figure 2, you will see a drawing showcasing the axes and the coordinate frames without the Y axis. We are omitting the Y axis in our drawing because they are not relevant to the DH representation. However, it is very simple to add the Y axis since we want to maintain a right-handed coordinate frame.

Step 2: Determining the DH parameters

The DH parameters are necessary to represent the transformation undergone from frame to frame. For instance, the DH method states that by rotating by an angle θ, Translating along the z-axis by d units, translating along the x-axis by a-units, and rotating along the x-axis by ɑ degrees, you can obtain the transformation matrix for a frame i-1 to i. The transformation matrices are represented by the letter A.

Therefore, to find the transformation matrices between frames we must find the DH parameters and evaluate them using the A matrix. Section 5 (Stick Plot Development)

        For the stick plot, we first substituted the symbolic intermediate matrices with the arm’s values, and for the last one we used the final transformation matrix. Then we derived the positions of the joints by pulling the values from the matrices. To create the arrows, we used the quiver3 function and created an arrow for each dimension of each joint. We calculated the magnitude of each unit vector and multiplied it by a constant of 30 for the x and y directions and 50 for the z direction so that the arrows would be easily visible.

Discussion

We successfully derived our forward kinematic transformation. The DH method is an elegant and systematic way of deriving a model with no edge cases. After deriving our symbolic matrices by hand, we coded functions such as dh2mat(), dh2fk(), and fk3001().

Originally, when we implemented dh2mat(), we used Matlab’s default trigonometric functions which were in radians. Through some error, we realized that we should have used matlab’s degree version of the functions. Additionally, when writing these functions, we struggled with understanding Matlab’s symbolic representation of matrices and how to use them properly. Again, through some error and research, we discovered how to use them. Despite our success, it would have been useful to have been explained how to use symbolic matrices in lab to avoid losing significant project time figuring them out.

As we can see from section 4, the arm is behaving fairly consistently, besides an outlier in the first iteration (which occurred on most of the runs we performed). The outlier could be caused by a lack of precise control over the arm's movement while it is still performing the kinematics calculations. To prevent this, a pause could be added in the movements before actually moving the arm to give the processor enough time to calculate the kinematics. The error seen is systemic, and caused by physical error between how much the motors are commanded to move and how much they actually move. The average end effector pose is accurate for the x-coordinate, but not the y or z-coordinate as shown through the RMS error. The RMS error is very high for the y-coordinate at 8.1716 because of the first value read, which is an outlier. This brought the average from around 1 to around -2. The average z-coordinate is less accurate than the average x-coordinate but still better than the y-coordinate, since it has an RMS error of 3.1143. The computed results did not differ from the actual position, the arm was accurately moving to the position we commanded it to, although it is much harder to tell exact values as humans compared to the feedback from the arm.

The most complicated part of part 5 of the lab was trying to understand how the quiver3 function works. The Matlab documentation provided hard-to-understand language and was not the most useful. Fortunately, after discussing some solutions with other groups and our teammates we found a solution: scaling the respective columns of a frame by an arbitrary constant to make the arrow large enough when compared to the scale of the position of the end effector. Additionally, we understood that the quiver3 function only plots one arrow at a time for a given position so this process had to be repeated several times for each coordinate axis. Lastly, the stick plot’s seen in the results section confirmed the correctness of our forward kinematics calculations.

Section 6 was difficult to gauge the success of our plotting at first. Because the robot changed positions and took up varying amounts on the workspace, the 3D plot we were using tried to adjust the scaling with each new piece of data it received. This made it difficult to follow what the plot was doing and determine whether it was properly plotting the arms motion. We solved this by fixing the scale to a set number that the robot was unlikely to reach outside of and this held the plot and scaling steady. We recommend other teams take the same steps we did to ensure an easy to follow visualization.

        While working on section 7, we stumbled upon the way to plot a trajectory. It involved calling the plot_arm function multiple times while the robot arm was in motion. Since we were initially replotting onto the same figure, the stages of the robot arm moving to the setpoint were plotted to create a visualization of the interpolation trajectory. The visual was helpful when going over trajectories so we would recommend adding this task to further display what a trajectory is and what it looks like in practice.

As mentioned in the Results section highlighting the 8th section of our lab, the trajectory of our robot does look triangular. However, it contains a rounded side. Additionally, the end effector may want to move along the negative z-axis linearly, but due to the actuators being revolute joints, the trajectory is curved. There is a difference between the desired motion in the joint space and the task space. In brief, our robot has revolute joints which make its path in the XZ plane of the task space a curved path despite desiring a linear motion.

Conclusion

In this lab, we developed the functionality for forward kinematics with our robot arm through several functions that convert joint positions into actual measurements. Additionally, we performed tests and plotted data to show the movement and frames of reference for the different parts of the arm. We found that there is a difference between the desired motion in the joint space and the task space through analysis of trajectory planning. For future versions of the lab, it could be helpful to instruct students to lock the scaling as we found in this lab, as well as overlaying multiple robot motions on the same plot to see the changes in movement clearly.
